### 3.8 Broader comparison: coordinate LiftOver and CAT

The seventeen-genome study above compares the three **homology lift-over** tools that take only a
reference genome, its annotation, and a target genome. Two widely-used tools sit *outside* that
input class, and a representative sub-study places LiftOn against them on equal, tool-neutral
footing: **coordinate LiftOver** (CrossMap projecting the reference annotation through a
chain) and the **Comparative Annotation Toolkit (CAT)**. Both are alignment-based: they require a
precomputed **whole-genome Cactus alignment** of the pair (LiftOver consumes a chain extracted
from it; CAT consumes the HAL directly). We built that alignment ourselves per pair and re-scored
every tool with the same parasail kernel used throughout. The sub-study was scoped to five
representative pairs, one per divergence tier; **the result is as much about feasibility as about
accuracy.**

**Where the alignment-based tools can run, LiftOn leads and LiftOver is out-of-class.** The
Cactus alignment converged only on the two least-divergent pairs — same-species maize B73→Mo17 and
close human→gorilla. There, LiftOn v1.0.9 has the highest re-scored protein identity (Table 11), as
in the main study, while coordinate LiftOver behaves exactly as its mechanism predicts: it lifts
**most features** (99.4% of reference coding transcripts on human→gorilla, 85.5% on the
structurally-variable maize pair) but its re-translated CDS scores **well below even the DNA-only
Liftoff** (0.920 vs 0.963 on gorilla; 0.841 vs 0.917 on maize) because coordinate projection does
**not correct frame**. Reported honestly as a *dual* metric — a high feature-lift rate and a low
protein identity (Fig. 7C) — LiftOver is a complementary coordinate tool, not a rival annotation
lift-over method: it answers "where does this interval land?", not "what is the correct gene model
on the target?".

**Where divergence is high — the regime that matters most — the alignment-based tools cannot run
at all.** On the three divergent pairs (dog→cat, *Arabidopsis*→rice, human→chicken), building the
required Cactus alignment was **intractable**. `cactus_consolidated` exhausts memory
super-linearly with divergence: a clean dog→cat subset run was issued **279 GB** for the
consolidation job and still aborted (`Cannot allocate memory`), and a final attempt at **700 GB
of memory on a 1 TB machine OOMed again** — a ~700× blow-up for a 380 MB subset, driven by the
divergent all-vs-all alignment graph (not by masking: the inputs are 46–49% soft-masked). dog→cat
is the *smallest* of the three divergent subsets, so the larger, more divergent *Arabidopsis*→rice
and human→chicken are at least as infeasible. CAT compounds this: even on the close human→gorilla
HAL it completed only 18 of 21 of its luigi tasks before its internal toil sub-workflows
(chaining, hint generation) failed, so CAT yields no scorable annotation here at all. The homology
lift-over tools, by contrast, **already ran on all three divergent pairs** as whole genomes
(Table 5: LiftOn v1.0.9 reaches 0.871 dog→cat, 0.551 *Arabidopsis*→rice, 0.774 human→chicken)
because they need only `(reference, reference-annotation, target)` and no precomputed alignment.

This is the sharpest statement of the input-class distinction that runs through the whole report.
The alignment-based comparison class — CAT and chain-based LiftOver — is **gated by a
whole-genome alignment that does not scale to divergence**, exactly the regime where annotation
transfer is hardest and a dual-evidence method is most valuable. LiftOn occupies the other class:
it builds its own DNA and protein evidence from the raw genomes, runs on every pair from
same-species to mammal→bird, and leads on accuracy wherever a head-to-head is even possible.
